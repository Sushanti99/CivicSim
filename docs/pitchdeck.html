CivicSim 02 / 15
~50 SEC · OPPORTUNITY

AI simulation is already standard practice across industries. Product teams test on synthetic users before launch. Market researchers run synthetic survey panels instead of real ones, saving weeks and thousands of dollars. Pharma, finance, advertising are all stress-testing with simulated populations.

But look at public policy. We still pass laws the old way. Important legislation ships without ever being tested against a synthetic version of the people it affects. We only find out whether it worked after it's already law.

Policy is the natural next frontier for this technology, and also the hardest, because representation isn't optional. If your simulation misses a community, you've automated a bias and called it data. That's the bar we built for.

CivicSim 03 / 15
~60 SEC · PROBLEM

There are three failure modes, and all three happen before the LLM generates a single word.

First: stereotype, not population. Prompting "simulate a 35-year-old voter" gives you the model's average caricature with no real demographic distribution behind it. Fine for ad copy, disqualifying for policy.

Second: survey panels aren't populations. They weren't designed to be representative. They were designed to answer specific research questions. We measured this: for young Black Americans, panels misplace 32 percent into the wrong income bracket.

Third: the wrong variables, confidently. Age, income, education sound important. But geography often explains more. Almost everyone skips it.

The bottom line on this slide: the model isn't the problem. The "who" is.

CivicSim 05 / 15
~40 SEC · INTRODUCING CIVICSIM

Three pillars, each a direct fix for one of the three problems I just walked through.

Pillar one: representative population. Every agent sampled from ACS Census microdata, 2.5 million records. The distributions match the actual demographics of wherever you picked.

Pillar two: policy-specific grounding. Every agent carries an empirical opinion prior from 38,449 real Pew respondents. The LLM isn't guessing. It's conditioned on what people like that have actually said.

Pillar three: fully inspectable. Every persona's demographics, prior, stance, and reasoning saved per run. Nothing is a black box.

We're not promising perfect prediction. The goal is representative, transparent simulation before real-world rollout.

CivicSim 06 / 15
~50 SEC · ARCHITECTURE

Quick technical pass for the engineers. Next.js frontend on Vercel, a thin UI layer that proxies to the backend. FastAPI on Fly.io is the orchestration layer.

Three core services underneath it. The agent sampler generates synthetic people using a largest-remainder algorithm that guarantees distributions match census. The prior lookup returns historical opinion distributions per demographic profile, with sparse-cell backoff when exact combinations are thin. The LLM client is model-agnostic and works with OpenAI, Anthropic, or local models.

Worth flagging: the whole pipeline uses Server-Sent Events to stream responses live. You see agents arrive in real time as they're generated, which matters for the transparency story. Users watch the system think, not wait for a finished result.

CivicSim 09 / 15
~55 SEC · HOW IT WORKS (CLICK THROUGH)

Four phases. Reveal each as you go.

(Step 1.) Sample. Build N agents matching the location's demographics. Largest-remainder algorithm, same method used in election apportionment. Proportions are guaranteed to match census.

(Step 2.) Prime. This is the step everyone else skips. We attach a real opinion prior to each agent: what people with these demographics have historically said about this type of question. The LLM doesn't guess. It conditions.

(Step 3.) Simulate. LLM generates stance and rationale for each agent, conditioned on both demographics and prior. You get a reasoning chain, not just a vote.

(Step 4.) Aggregate. Results stream live, group divergence surfaced. Not just "68% support" but renters vs. homeowners, urban vs. rural, wherever the real signal is.

CivicSim 10 / 15
~40 SEC · DATA FOUNDATION

Two datasets. Both public, both gold-standard, both used in academic policy research for decades.

ACS on the left: 2.5 million adult records a year via IPUMS. The critical thing is it's the only public source with joint demographic distributions. Most datasets tell you how many people are Black and how many are low-income. ACS tells you how many are both. That's what preserves intersectional groups.

Pew ATP on the right: 38,449 respondents, 80+ waves, 2021 to 2024. Consistent methodology, broad topic coverage. We compile it into an opinion-prior lookup with no PII, everything aggregated.

Census for the who. Pew for what they think. Our contribution is how we combine them.

CivicSim 11 / 15
~60 SEC · RESULTS · ★ KEY SLIDE

If you remember one slide from this talk, this is it.

We ran 1,426 opinion items across 14 policy domains, testing every combination of 7 demographic variables, about 180,000 mini-experiments total.

10.6%: variance explained by the textbook demographic variables. Age, income, education. What every research team starts with.

25.2%: variance explained when we let the data tell us which variables actually matter per policy domain. Same models. Same compute. Smarter conditioning.

2.4×: not a marginal improvement. We're more than doubling explanatory power without touching the LLM.

And the punchline: Census division, which region of the country you live in, drives a 53.5% signal drop when removed. The most important variable. The one the textbook approach throws out. That's the finding we want to leave with you.

CivicSim 12 / 15
~45 SEC · EVALS

We validated CivicSim against real held-out Pew ATP data, comparing simulated opinion distributions to what real respondents actually said.

Two metrics on the slide. TVD of 0.245: Total Variation Distance, lower is better. That sits in the "moderate" calibration zone. The threshold for excellent is below 0.15; we're in the 0.15 to 0.30 band. Wasserstein of 0.219 is the ordinal-aware metric. It respects the structure of Likert responses, so moving from "somewhat favor" to "strongly favor" costs less than crossing the full scale.

Tested across Economy and Politics & Government domains. Each question: 30 agents, 20 demographic slices, marginals first then cross-cuts, for 600 simulated respondents per question. Wasserstein is the preferred metric for Likert-scale data.

Moderate calibration is honest and expected for a system this early. The purpose isn't to claim perfection. It's to show that the distributions are in the right neighborhood and we have a rigorous eval framework to improve from.

CivicSim 14 / 15
~45 SEC · LIMITS & NEXT

We want to be honest about what CivicSim is and isn't. In policy work, overclaiming is worse than underclaiming.

Data limits: v1 covers Alameda County only. Some survey time periods are sparse. Smallest intersectional subgroups are still thin. We use backoff, but it's a real constraint.

Simulation limits: this is directional realism, not prediction. We tell you the structure of disagreement: who supports, who opposes, where subgroups diverge. We can't tell you the exact percentage when the policy ships. Opinion dynamics over time aren't modeled yet.

Where we're heading: multi-state expansion, longitudinal validation against real outcomes, open-sourcing the variable-selection toolkit.

Pause on the warning box at the bottom. Representative simulations support decisions. They never replace the voices of real communities. That's the moral spine of this project.
