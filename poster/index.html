Our Solution

CivicSim replaces biased panels with census-grounded synthetic agents conditioned on evidence-selected demographics.

We separate who people are (anchored to U.S. Census ACS microdata) from what they are likely to think (grounded in 38,449 Pew ATP respondents across 79 survey waves). Feature selection is driven by information gain, not convention — so every agent carries the traits that actually predict opinion.

Step 01

Start with a realistic population.

Build agents from Census microdata so the simulated public mirrors real demographics.

Step 02

Choose traits that actually improve prediction.

Use evidence-based feature selection instead of relying on default demographic bundles.

Step 03

Use geography where it matters most.

Apply location detail by topic, adding finer granularity for younger groups when needed.

Background

In 2024, American polls produced the largest demographic prediction error in four decades of modern survey research.

2024 Pre-election polls predicted Harris would win Hispanic voters by 20+ points and young voters (18–29) by 28–31 points. On election night, those projections collapsed. Hispanic men swung 33 points toward Trump in four years — from Biden +23 in 2020 to Trump +10 in 2024. Among voters under 30, polls missed by 17–27 percentage points; Harris won that group by just 4–11 points instead of the promised supermajority.

Latino men swing, 2020→2024 Biden won Latino men by 23 pts; Trump won them by 10. The largest four-year demographic swing measured in modern U.S. elections.

These weren't random noise. Polls systematically under-sampled lower-income, non-college, and rural Hispanic respondents — the exact sub-groups whose behavior changed most. The same income and geography blind spots reappear election cycle after election cycle because survey convenience panels misrepresent who is actually in America. Phone-survey response rates have fallen from 36% in 1997 to under 6% today, concentrating respondents among the engaged, educated, and affluent — and making these errors structural, not accidental.

Problem

Polls use the wrong people and the wrong variables — and both errors compound.

Survey convenience panels over-represent high-income, college-educated, coastal respondents. Then, even within those skewed samples, analysts condition on age, race, and gender — the traits that feel intuitive but carry less than 11% of available opinion signal. The two most predictive variables — income bracket and census division — are routinely ignored.

Proof of Problem

Proof 1: Survey-based populations can miss or flatten key communities.

Representation Gap

Even after statistical weighting, some communities are still represented inaccurately. The worst case: TVD = 0.42 for Black, rural, low-income respondents — meaning 42% of their opinion probability mass lands in the wrong census division when income bias goes uncorrected.

Proof 2: Common demographic inputs leave useful predictive information unused.

Feature Gap

The greedy path reaches 0.73 nats. The conventional path reaches only 0.11 nats.

Age + race + education — the default polling bundle — captures less than 11% of available opinion signal. Income + geography captures 6× more.

Census division contributes 47% of joint predictive power. Income bracket contributes 46%.

Together, these two variables explain 93% of all opinion predictability — yet neither appears in standard demographic weighting schemes.

Proof 3: The importance of location changes by topic.

Geography Gap

Place matters a lot for some issues and very little for others. International affairs and immigration retain strong geographic signal even after demographic conditioning; technology and environment transfer cleanly without it. One-size-fits-all geography assumptions introduce avoidable error.

Evaluation Framework

We evaluate representation, prediction quality, and when location adds value.

  • TVD
    Representation Check Measures how closely a modeled population matches real-world population distributions.
  • IG
    Predictive Value Check Measures which demographic inputs truly improve opinion prediction across policy questions.
  • JS
    Geography Relevance Check Measures when location still changes predicted opinions after accounting for demographics.
Results / Conclusion

What we learned for building public-facing opinion simulators.

  • 01
    Ground population in Census, not convenience panels. ACS microdata eliminates the income over-representation gap that caused 2024's largest prediction errors. Survey panels can't be re-weighted out of structural absence.
  • 02
    Select features by information gain, not convention. Replacing age + race + gender with income + geography increases opinion predictability 6× with no added model complexity.
  • 03
    Apply geography selectively by domain. Census division conditioning is required for international affairs and immigration; it adds noise for technology and environment. Topic-aware geography beats always-on.
